More Clicks ≠ More Business

A pilot A/B test of two ad campaigns. The new creative doubled click-through rate — but a full-funnel statistical analysis showed it drove no additional sales, preventing a costly scale-up.

Role

Marketing Analyst

Context

Marketing A/B test · pilot

Stack

Python · Claude

Impact

~$20K wasted spend prevented

A statistical test is only useful when it changes a business decision. Here, the right tests turned a tempting "ship it" into a defensible hold — stopping a planned ~$20K scale-up before the money was committed.

The problem

A retailer ran a one-month pilot of a new ad creative against its existing one. The new "Test" campaign was winning clicks — and the plan was to scale it across the full budget.

But clicks aren't the goal; customers are. The real question: Does the new campaign drive more business, or just win the eye-catching metric? This analysis tests every stage of the funnel — and counts a win only if it's both statistically real and commercially meaningful.

The approach

The two campaigns were treated as independent samples and compared across every funnel metric. But first I set a metric hierarchy — so the go/no-go decision rested on commercial outcomes, not on whichever number happened to look good.

Step 1 — Test each variable for normality

For every numeric metric I ran a Shapiro-Wilk test and a Q-Q plot, on Control and Test separately. The rule: If both groups are normal, use the parametric test (Welch's t); if even one isn't, drop to the non-parametric test (Mann-Whitney). Proportions like CTR and conversion skip this step — normality is irrelevant for a rate.

Step 2 — Match the test to each variable

The test follows the form of the data, not the metric's name. Each variable was classified by type and distribution, then routed to the test its structure demands:

VariableData typeDistributionApproachTest
CTRProportion (rate)n/a — CLT—Two-proportion z-test
Conversion rateProportion (rate)n/a — CLT—Two-proportion z-test
# View ContentCountNormal (both)ParametricWelch's t-test
# Website ClicksCountNon-normalNon-parametricMann-Whitney U
# SearchesCountNon-normalNon-parametricMann-Whitney U
# Add to CartCountNon-normalNon-parametricMann-Whitney U
# PurchaseCountNon-normalNon-parametricMann-Whitney U
CPAContinuous (cost)Right-skewedNon-parametricMann-Whitney U

Proportions are pooled across all impressions, so the test draws on hundreds of thousands of trials for maximum power. Independence of the daily series was confirmed (Ljung-Box), and all tests were two-tailed.

Why the right test matters commercially: Had I run a t-test on every metric and skipped the checks, a metric could have flagged "significant" by chance — making the campaign look like a winner it isn't. Acting on a false positive means scaling it and spending more for no return. Matching the test to each variable is what keeps a possible false win from becoming a real cost.

How AI shaped the work

I used Claude as an analytical thinking partner — not just a faster way to write code. Its highest-value contributions were about method and judgment:

  • Test selection — classifying each variable's data form and routing it to the correct test, then building the decision table above.
  • Reading stats against the commercial figures — holding each statistical result next to the movement of the revenue metrics (purchases, conversion), so a flashy traffic number never gets mistaken for a business result.
  • Independent verification — recomputing the headline figures from the raw data to confirm they were correct and internally consistent.

Where AI helps beyond speed: The biggest gain wasn't faster code — it was a reasoning reviewer that pressure-tested whether the right method was used, whether assumptions held, and whether the conclusion was honest. Used this way, AI is an always-on analytics mentor and devil's advocate, not an autocomplete.

The funnel reads cleanly: Test got more clicks but the same number of purchases — so its conversion rate is significantly lower, and add-to-cart dropped 27%. The new creative pulled in more clicks but lower-intent visitors — people who browse and bounce, not buy. The fix isn't to scale it; it's to retarget that traffic and tighten the audience before spending more.

Financial implication

On the strength of its click numbers, the team was ready to scale the Test creative — a planned ~$20K increase in spend behind it. The pilot's full-funnel verdict changed that call before the money was committed.

Because Test won more clicks but converted them at a significantly lower rate, that extra ~$20K would have bought more traffic without more sales. So the first move was to stop the scale-up — then redirect the budget to retarget Test's traffic, where its lower-intent visitors could be converted instead of just paid for.

A doubled click-through rate is not a business case. The new campaign produced no additional sales and a significantly worse conversion rate — so the recommendation was to hold it, not scale it.

Reflection

The discipline that mattered: A metric going up is not, by itself, a win. Reading the entire funnel — not just the flashy top-line metric — turned an obvious "ship it" into a defensible "hold." The most valuable output of an analysis isn't the p-value; it's the judgment to separate a real, meaningful effect from a flattering one, and to translate it into the commercial decision it implies.

Note: One Control day was dropped for a missing value during cleaning (Control, 29 valid days; Test, 30). As all comparisons use rates, medians, or means — and the tests handle unequal sample sizes — this does not bias the results.
