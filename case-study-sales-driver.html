What's really driving sales?

An interactive dashboard that decomposes sales volume by brand, district, city and customer — turning a flat 2% topline into a clear map of what moved, and why.

Role

Sales Analyst

Context

FMCG · Business Development

Stack

Power BI

Impact

Self-Service Sales Driver Analysis

2% growth can hide a hundred different stories. This dashboard pulls volume apart — brand by brand, district by district, account by account — so one topline number becomes a short list of exactly what to protect and what to fix.

The problem

Leadership could see the topline move month to month — but answering why meant hours of manual pivoting. Which brands gained? Which districts slipped? Was a swing a real trend or just a noisy month? By the time the answer arrived, the moment to act had often passed.

The brief was deceptively simple: build one report that lets anyone see the drivers of sales volume — by city, district, brand and customer — and instantly compare each against the previous period and the same period last year.

The approach

I designed the report around a single idea: every visual should answer one driver question, and the two time comparisons should separate noise from trend.

1 · Model

One clean fact table

Sales Volume by Date,
segmented by:

  • District
  • City
  • Brand
  • Customer
2 · Measures

Two driver lenses

  • Vs P-1 — prior month
  • Vs P-12 — same month last year
3 · Design

One question per visual

  • Where — city & district ranking
  • What — brand breakdown
  • Who — customer driver table

The two comparison measures are the heart of it. Vs P-1 (previous period) catches short-term swings; Vs P-12 (same period last year) strips out seasonality to expose the underlying trend. A brand can look fine month-on-month yet be structurally declining year-on-year — only the second lens reveals it.

Built in Power BI

The report was built and delivered in Power BI Desktop — data model, the Vs P-1 / Vs P-12 measures, and native visuals only. The interactive version that follows is a faithful web rebuild so it can live on this site without a Power BI account.

What it reveals

Across Q4, total volume grew a quiet +2.3% year-on-year (783,913 → 801,667 units). On its own that number says "steady." The dashboard says otherwise: underneath it is a clear rotation — a handful of brands and districts surging while others quietly erode.

The concentration risk: the top 10 of 34 customers account for 42% of volume — and even there the trend splits, with Peak Pantry (+13.3%) and MetroMunch (+12.6%) growing while FamilyFare (−7.4%) and Cornerstone (−4.3%) decline. A flat topline was masking exactly the accounts to defend.

Conclusion

The dashboard turned a recurring "what happened to volume?" scramble into a 30-second, self-service answer. Instead of waiting on a manual pull, a category manager can land on the page, filter to their district or account, and read the drivers — with the trend (P-12) sitting right next to the blip (P-1) so they don't overreact to either.

Next steps

The point of finding the drivers is to act on both ends of the rotation — not just celebrate the topline. The data splits the next move cleanly into two plays.

Track 1 · Offense

Scale the growth drivers

Concentrate investment behind the brands, districts and accounts that are already gaining share.

  • Secure supply and shelf for the engine brands — Fizzora (+14.5%), HyperQuench (+18.3%) and NovaFizz (+17.6%) — so availability never constrains demand.
  • Replicate the leading-district playbook from South West (+8.3%), Central (+13.3%) and North East (+5.4%) in underperforming markets.
  • Deepen the high-growth accounts — Peak Pantry (+13.3%) and MetroMunch (+12.6%) — through priority ranging and joint business planning.
Track 2 · Defense

Arrest the declines

Diagnose and contain the eroding brands, districts and accounts before they weigh down the topline.

  • Investigate the declining brands — ChillMist (−12.1%), CitrusFlare (−5.4%), FlipFizz (−7.6%) and BoldBerry (−6.7%) — to isolate pricing, distribution or demand drivers.
  • Develop recovery plans for the softening districts, Paris (−3.0%, the second-largest market) and West (−4.6%), with focused field and trade investment.
  • Prioritise retention for at-risk accounts FamilyFare (−7.4%) and Cornerstone (−4.3%) ahead of competitive encroachment.

The discipline: don't let a flat 2% lull the business. The same dashboard that surfaces the rotation is the tool to monitor whether these two tracks are working — month after month.

A dashboard's job isn't to display the number — it's to point at the decision behind it. A flat 2% was hiding a rotation worth acting on.

Data note: figures are from an illustrative French FMCG distribution dataset (fictional brands and customers) covering Q4 (Oct–Dec) 2022 and 2023 — 2,850 records across 24 brands, 34 customers, 31 cities and 6 sales districts. Safe to share publicly.
